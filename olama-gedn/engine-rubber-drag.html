How it works

Physics-based pitch. Not parameter-based.

Rubber Drag models three physical properties of elastic materials — not three DSP parameters. The difference is audible.

01

Elastic Tension Simulation

A spring-mass model drives the pitch envelope. Tension sets the stiffness of the "material". Higher tension = faster, more aggressive drag. Lower tension = slow, lazy, syrupy pull. It behaves like a physical property, not a knob.

model: spring_mass | tension_range: 10%–100%
02

Material Memory Hysteresis

Each triggered hit inherits pitch state from the previous hit with a decay curve. Like a rubber band that doesn't return fully to neutral — the material remembers being stretched. Consecutive hits develop cumulative character.

memory_decay: 0.2–8.0s | hysteresis: active
03

Snap Overshoot Model

When material releases, it doesn't just return to zero — it overshoots and oscillates. The Snap parameter controls overshoot amplitude. At high settings, every note has a micro-pitch bounce at the end that sounds like a physical pluck.

overshoot_range: 0–200% | oscillations: 1–3 cycles

Common questions

Is this a pitch modulator or a time-stretching engine?
Both. Rubber Drag combines pitch modulation with time-domain processing — the elastic behavior affects both pitch and timing simultaneously, just like physical elastic material does. It's not a separate pitch plugin; it's integrated into the time-stretching pipeline for a unified physical character.
Will the organic variation make my audio unpredictable?
The variation range is controlled by the Tension and Memory parameters. At low values, every hit is nearly identical. As you increase them, each hit develops more unique character. You're always in control of the variance floor and ceiling.
Can I use this on full mix buses or just individual instruments?
Both work. On individual instruments (bass, drums, chops), the physical character is most pronounced. On full buses, you get a subtle elastic warmth that's reminiscent of the whole-mix elastic feel of tape. Start with individual instruments first to understand the character.
