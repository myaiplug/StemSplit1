How it works

Every grain is its own universe.

Three independent processes govern grain behavior — creating textures too complex to simulate manually, too organic to mistake for effects.

01

Stochastic Grain Scheduling

Rather than extracting grains at fixed intervals, Granular Drift uses a Poisson arrival model — each grain's start position is drawn from a probability distribution around the playback position. The scatter parameter controls the standard deviation of this distribution, from tight (coherent texture) to loose (cloud-like randomness).

grain_size: 8–800ms | scatter: 0–100% | env: gaussian/trapezoid
02

Per-Grain Pitch Drift

Each active grain receives an independent pitch offset drawn from a Gaussian distribution centered at the target pitch. The drift range sets the distribution's standard deviation — at 0% you get standard granular; at 100% you get a ±24 semitone pitch cloud. The curve parameter controls whether the distribution is symmetric or skewed toward the root.

drift_range: ±0–24st | curve: gaussian | per_grain: true
03

Grain Density Morphing

The density parameter sets how many simultaneous grains are active at any moment. At 4 grains you hear individual sound fragments — sparse, staccato. At 512 grains the texture becomes dense and continuous, indistinguishable from a synthesized pad. The crossfade mode determines how grains overlap when density is adjusted live.

density: 4–512 grains | crossfade: spectral | live_morph: true

Common questions

Is Granular Drift for live performance or only offline processing?
Both. In Live mode, Granular Drift processes audio in real-time — you can automate grain size, scatter, and density as the track plays. In Offline mode, it renders higher-quality textures with larger grain buffers than real-time allows. For extreme grain counts (256+), offline render at 96kHz is recommended for the highest quality output.
Does the stochastic model mean every render sounds different?
Yes — by design. The Poisson grain scheduler uses a seed value that changes each render. You can lock the seed for repeatable output if your workflow requires it, or let it vary for unique renders. Most users prefer the variation: two passes through the same settings produce complementary, layerable textures.
How is this different from a regular granular synthesizer like Ableton's sampler?
Standard granular synths use fixed pitch per grain and regular (or random uniform) grain extraction. Granular Drift uses Gaussian pitch distribution per grain and Poisson arrival timing — these are qualitatively different models. The result is that Drift sounds biological; standard granular sounds mechanical. The density range (up to 512 grains) also far exceeds most sampler implementations.
